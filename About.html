About Kylie

Kylie has always loved stories. Growing up in a family of storytellers and booklovers, she quickly learned the magic of words —how they could comfort, entertain, and open up whole new worlds. Even as a child, she found joy in making up her own tales and getting lost in the ones she read.

Her roots trace back to the United Kingdom, where her grandparents—survivors of World War II—shared vivid accounts of a time and place both distant and deeply connected to her own life. Their stories ignited her imagination and sparked a lifelong love for narrative.

A mother of four, Kylie is a credited screenwriter, the published author of Thaddeus Bix and the Pirates of Pepperjack, and a contributor to 365 Ways to Connect with Your Soul. Her true passion, however, lies in writing dual-timeline women’s historical fiction, often set in the late Victorian era or amid the upheaval of the World Wars. Her writing tends to explore themes of love, loss, and resilience, primarily through the lens of complex female protagonists navigating hardship. She is especially drawn to the forgotten details of the past—fragments of life and culture that have faded in today’s fast-paced world. In her work, Kylie brings together her love of the past, her fascination with human emotion and connection, and her enduring affection for both England and Australia.

Kylie is currently pursuing a Bachelor of Arts in Creative Writing at Flinders University. When she’s not writing, studying, or attending inspiring experiences like the Fiona McIntosh Masterclass, you’ll likely find her lost in a good book, exploring the outdoors, travelling, or simply enjoying time with her family.

‘For me, there is nothing quite like the moment a story arrives—that spark of magic and mystery that feels both otherworldly and deeply human. When I accept it, step into it, and begin to translate its essence onto the page—with all its emotion, imagery, humor, and drama—I’m filled with a sense of wonder. It becomes the prelude to something greater. Because as powerful as receiving a story can be, there is something equally profound in setting it free. That moment of release is beautifully bittersweet—when the story is no longer mine alone, but like a wish sent out into the world, finding its way into the hearts of others.’
